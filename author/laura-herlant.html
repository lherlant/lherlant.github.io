A* on Manifolds

 Fri 17 January 2020 by Laura Herlant

While I was at NeurIPS 2019, I sat down in a crowded cafe between sessions. I shared a table and a conversation with another conference attendee, and we got to talking about half-baked ideas. He described one such idea he had, about approximating curvature in a multi-dimensional space. Essentially, you start at a point on a manifold, and want to find where a straight line path from that point intersects the manifold again. The catch being that you only have access to local information about your current location (position and curvature). Don't ask me what real world problem this applies to. But it seemed to me it was a rewording of the shortest-path problem.

When I was back at the hotel that night, I was unwinding so wrote a cute little implementation of that well-beloved A* algorithm -- now on a 3-D surface (generalizes to N-D, but harder to visualize). I generated points from some simple surfaces to test out - a sphere, an ellipsoid, and an hyperboloid.

A cool thing I learned while doing this is to use the Fibonacci lattice to get evenly distributed points for the sphere. I used the same technique for the ellipsoid and hyperboloid, and it makes for a much nicer visualization than other ways of randomly sampling points.

In general, I don't see why A* wouldn't expand to higher dimensions or more complex surfaces. The Euclidean distance between two points is an admissible heuristic, and distances between any two given adjacent points are always positive, so A* will yield an optimal solution.
